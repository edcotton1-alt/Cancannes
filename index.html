01Motion · The Machine

"This House believes the machine will never build a brand."

The defining argument of the year, put to the House. For the motion: Marc Pritchard, who spends $10bn a year and insists the machine can make everything except the one thing that matters. Against: Demis Hassabis, who built the machine and knows exactly what it can already do. The head of a frontier lab against the most powerful brand-builder alive, on a single sentence the room must vote up or down.

The ClashPritchard's case: a machine can generate a million ads by lunch and not one brand, because a brand is meaning and meaning is human. Hassabis's reply: the machine never claimed to — it builds the thousand drafts a human picks from, and P&G already runs on those tools. So the House must decide what a brand even is before it can vote.
Motion falls — Ayes 41%, Noes 59%
The House sides with Hassabis

Pritchard throws the best line of the night. Hassabis slips it by agreeing. The machine doesn't build the brand — it builds the thousand drafts a human picks from, and P&G already runs on those tools. You can't carry a motion against a man standing where you already stand. Pritchard wins the quote. The House votes the motion down.

02Motion · The Law

"This House believes the work this industry celebrates never reaches the public."

The motion that puts Cannes itself on trial. For: Mark Penn, the pollster who's measured what real people actually notice — and it isn't this. Against: Sir John Hegarty, who has spent 40 years calling advertising an art form and won't see it reduced to reach. The numbers against the church of the idea, resolved to a single vote.

The ClashPenn's case: the polling is brutal — the work this festival crowns is invisible to the people it's supposedly for. Hegarty's reply: reach is not the measure; a single great idea outlives a thousand seen-and-forgotten ones. The House must choose whether to score advertising by who saw it or by what it was.
Motion carries — Ayes 54%, Noes 46%
The House sides with Penn, narrowly
Better case PennBest speech HegartyThe Division Ayes

Hegarty defends craft so beautifully the room aches for him. That's the trap. Penn doesn't argue taste — he argues reach, and he has the polling: the work Cannes worships is invisible to the people it's for. Hegarty wins every heart in the building. The building is the problem. The finest speech loses to the harder number — the motion carries by the margin of a guilty conscience.

03Motion · The Soul

"This House believes a brand the world loves owes the world more."

Both speakers believe brand love is the most powerful force in marketing — they split on what it obliges. For: Stella McCartney, who holds that love is borrowed trust and the more you're adored, the more you owe. Against: Manolo Arroyo, who runs the most-loved brand on earth and treats that affection as a renewable licence. Not "should you scale" — the question they'd genuinely divide on.

Arroyo's reply: affection is earned and renewable, worth more than any single campaign, and Coke proves love survives almost anything. The House decides whether being adored is a permission slip or a higher obligation.
Motion carries — Ayes 71%, Noes 29%
The House sides with McCartney, decisively
Better case McCartneyHardest brief ArroyoThe Division Ayes

Arroyo's position is honest: love is earned, renewable, worth more than any campaign. Coke proves love survives almost anything. But he's defending the idea that being loved excuses you — to a room whose entire faith is that a brand means something. McCartney doesn't attack his love. She agrees it's real, then turns it into the charge: a billion people trust you, and you spent the trust on plastic. Arroyo says love is a licence. The House rules it's collateral.

04Motion · The Crowd

"This House believes reach is not creativity."

The festival's deepest fault line, put to a vote. For: Chaka Sobhani, who runs creative for one of the world's most awarded networks and argues reach without craft is just noise at scale. Against: Dhar Mann, who reaches more humans before breakfast than most Grand Prix winners reach in a year — and calls that, plainly, the work. Volume's new empire against the keepers of the craft.

Mann's reply: a billion humans is not nothing, and a Lion most of them will never see is the industry talking to itself. The House must decide whether creativity is what moves the most people or what moves them most.
Motion carries — Ayes 58%, Noes 42%
The House sides with Sobhani — on home turf
Better case MannOwns the definition SobhaniThe Division Ayes

Mann answers the clash better. He puts a billion humans on the table and dares Sobhani to match it — "they'll never see your Lion" lands clean. He owns scale. But the House is the House: a creativity festival, full of people whose whole identity is that attention and art are different things. Sobhani doesn't out-reach him. She makes moving someone deeply the rarer feat, and she's spent a career proving it. Mann owns the scoreboard. Sobhani owns the definition — and the House writes the dictionary.

05Motion · The Operator

"This House believes no dataset can originate a great idea."

The fault line under every other motion: is an idea a human act, or just a variable to optimize? For: Richard Dickson, who bet Gap's whole turnaround on a feeling no dashboard predicted — Young Miko, Victoria Beckham, choreographed denim — and moved the stock and the culture. Against: David Sandström, who rebuilt Klarna into a factory running AI for 80% of its copy and says the monopoly on creativity has fallen.

Sandström's reply: Gap can't A/B-test its way out of a sales miss, most "storytelling" is expensive guessing, and the machine already does the work. The House votes on whether the idea is the one thing that can't be automated.

Sandström wins the efficiency argument cold: most "storytelling" is expensive guessing, and the factory is real. Then the clash turns on him. No dashboard told Gap to reimagine a Young Miko video — data optimizes what exists, it can't originate what doesn't. And he indicts himself: he says the product builds the brand, consistency beats campaigns. That's a creative's faith in an engineer's coat. Sandström wins the factory. Dickson wins what the factory makes — and a House of creatives won't vote for its own automation. In a room of CFOs, the motion falls.
