01The Machine

The Brand vs. The Machine

The defining argument of the year, in its purest form: the head of a frontier AI lab against the most powerful brand-builder alive, on whether the machine can do the actual job. No other pairing on the roster makes the collision this total — the one who builds the tool vs. the one who spends $10bn a year insisting it isn't enough.

Argument HassabisForcing Q PritchardThe Room Hassabis

Pritchard throws the best line of the night. Hassabis slips it by agreeing. The machine doesn't build the brand — it builds the thousand drafts a human picks from, and P&G already runs on those tools. You can't land a punch on a man standing where you already stand. Pritchard wins the quote. Hassabis wins the fight.

02The Law

The Data vs. The Faith

The fight over whether the work the industry crowns ever reaches a living human — and now Penn gets the opponent he was built to face. Not a fellow data man who'd half-agree, but the high priest of craft himself: the pollster who says the public never saw your masterpiece, against the man who's spent 40 years calling advertising an art form. 03The Soul

The Love vs. The Licence

Both of these people believe brand love is the most powerful force in marketing — they just disagree, violently, on what it obliges you to do. Arroyo runs the most-loved brand on earth and treats that affection as a renewable licence: people forgive Coke the plastic because they love it. McCartney's entire bet is the inverse — that love is borrowed trust, and the more people love you, the more you owe them. Not "should you scale," which she'd never want, but the question they'd actually fight over: is being adored a permission slip or a debt?

Blue corner

Forcing QArroyo, a billion people love your brand enough to forgive it being the most-audited plastic polluter on earth — you've turned affection into a permission slip. McCartney says that much love is a debt you owe back, not a licence to keep going. So whoever's right: does being adored let you off the hook, or chain you to a higher one?
McCartney def. Arroyo — on what love obliges
Method · won the room, won the argument
Argument McCartneyForcing Q McCartneyThe Room McCartney

Arroyo's position is honest: love is earned, renewable, worth more than any campaign. Coke proves love survives almost anything. But he's defending the idea that being loved excuses you — and he has to say it to a room whose entire faith is that a brand means something. McCartney doesn't attack his love. She agrees it's real, then turns it into the charge: a billion people trust you, and you spent the trust on plastic. Arroyo says love is a licence. McCartney proves it's collateral.

04The Crowd

The Creators vs. The Creatives

The festival's deepest fault line, finally in one ring: the two cultures that spent all week eyeing each other across the Croisette. Mann reaches more humans before breakfast than most Grand Prix winners reach in a year — with stories the craft establishment won't call advertising, or even good. Sobhani runs creative for one of the world's most awarded networks and has spent a career on the idea that reach without craft is just noise at scale. Volume's new empire vs. the keepers of the craft.

Forcing QMann gets a billion views on work this room would never award. Sobhani gives Lions to work most of those billion will never see. So settle it: is creativity what moves the most people, or what moves them most — and which of you is actually scared the other one's right?
Sobhani def. Mann — on the harder craft
Method · split, won the room
Argument SobhaniForcing Q MannThe Room Sobhani

Mann answers the question better. He puts a billion humans on the table and dares Sobhani to match it — "they'll never see your Lion" lands clean. He owns scale. Nobody in that room reaches what he reaches. But the room is the room: a creativity festival, full of people whose whole identity is that attention and art are different things. Sobhani doesn't out-reach him. She makes moving someone deeply the rarer feat — and she's spent a career proving it. Mann owns the scoreboard. Sobhani owns the definition. At Cannes, the definition is the home crowd.

05The Operator

The Story vs. The Spreadsheet

The fault line under every other fight: does culture get moved by an idea, or is the idea just a variable you optimize? Dickson bet Gap's entire turnaround on storytelling — Young Miko, Victoria Beckham, choreographed denim — and made the stock and the culture move. Sandström rebuilt Klarna into a marketing factory that runs AI for 80% of its copy, cut agency spend a quarter, and says the monopoly on creativity has fallen. The CEO who believes a great story sells the clothes vs. the CMO who believes the data doesn't lie.

Red corner
DEF.Richard Dickson
Gap Inc. · President & CEO
The Storyteller
Bet $15bn on getting back to storytelling
VS
Blue corner

Forcing QDickson, you turned a dying brand around with a music video and a feeling no dashboard predicted. Sandström, you'd have A/B-tested that video into a different video. So put it plainly: when the data and the gut disagree on the idea that actually moves culture, which one of you is about to be automated — and which one can a machine never replace?
Dickson def. Sandström — on the thing data can't see
Method · split, won the room
Argument EvenForcing Q DicksonThe Room Dickson

Sandström wins the efficiency argument cold: Gap can't A/B-test its way out of a sales miss, and most "storytelling" is expensive guessing. Then the question turns on him. No dashboard told Gap to reimagine a Young Miko video — data optimizes what exists, it can't originate what doesn't. And he indicts himself: he says the product builds the brand, consistency beats campaigns. That's a creative's faith in an engineer's coat. Sandström wins the factory. Dickson wins what the factory makes. A room of creatives won't vote for their own automation.
