01The Machine

The Brand vs. The Machine

The defining argument of the year, in its purest form: the head of a frontier AI lab against the most powerful brand-builder alive, on whether the machine can do the actual job. No other pairing on the roster makes the collision this total — the one who builds the tool vs. the one who spends $10bn a year insisting it isn't enough.

Forcing QYour machine can make a million ads by lunch. Pritchard says it can't build one brand. One of you is wrong about what a brand even is — so define it, out loud, and watch the other flinch.
Hassabis def. Pritchard — split decision
Method · argument over the room
Argument HassabisForcing Q PritchardThe Room Hassabis

Pritchard lands the line of the night — "robots can't build brands" beats anything Hassabis says aloud. But he's defending a wall already moving, and Hassabis disarms him by agreeing: the machine doesn't build the brand, it builds the thousand drafts a human chooses from — and P&G already runs on those tools. The punch has no power behind it because the target conceded the ground years ago. Pritchard wins the quote; Hassabis wins the fight.

02The Law

The Data vs. The Faith

The fight over whether the work the industry crowns ever reaches a living human — and now Penn gets the opponent he was built to face. Not a fellow data man who'd half-agree, but the high priest of craft himself: the pollster who says the public never saw your masterpiece, against the man who's spent 40 years calling advertising an art form. Numbers against the church of the idea.

Forcing QHegarty, you've called this an art form your whole life. Penn has the polling that says the public never saw the art — they saw nothing at all. So name the campaign from this festival that a normal person could describe a week later, or admit the gallery's been empty the whole time.
Penn def. Hegarty — by the numbers he brought
Method · argument-led, hostile room
Argument PennForcing Q PennThe Room Hegarty

Now Penn has a real adversary — not a scientist who'd concede the same point for different reasons, but the most eloquent believer in the room. Hegarty will defend craft so beautifully the audience aches for him to be right; nobody makes the case for the single great idea better. But that's exactly the trap. Penn doesn't argue taste, he argues reach — and he can show that the work Cannes worships is invisible to the people it's supposedly for. Hegarty wins every heart in the building, because the building is full of people who do what he does. Penn wins the one thing the building can't refute: the data that says nobody outside it noticed. The applause goes to the priest; the verdict goes to the pollster.

03The Soul

Conscience vs. The Bottle

What the industry is actually for — staged against a real material adversary, not an abstraction. The designer who built a global brand by refusing the easy, dirty, profitable thing, against the marketing chief of the company that tops the world's plastic-polluter audits year after year and just quietly walked back its own sustainability targets. Not conscience vs. capital in theory — conscience vs. the most beloved disposable object on earth.

Red corner
DEF.Stella McCartney
Founder & Creative Director
The Conscience
Built a brand on what she won't do
VS
Blue corner
Manolo ArroyoDEF.
The Coca-Cola Company · Global CMO
The Most Loved Bottle
World's #1 brand · world's #1 plastic polluter
Forcing QYour company is the most-loved brand on the planet and the most-audited plastic polluter on it, and last year it quietly cut its own recycling targets. So tell the room to its face: is "the most loved" worth anything if it's also the most thrown away — and would Stella's brand survive doing what yours does every single second?
McCartney def. Arroyo — by a mile
Method · won the room, won the argument
Argument McCartneyForcing Q McCartneyThe Room McCartney

This is the mismatch the soul of the festival deserves — and the one fight on the card where the better argument and the better room are the same person. Arroyo is a brilliant marketer with an impossible brief: defend the most beloved product on earth while standing on the year it walked back its plastic promises. He'll reach for "scale," "complexity," "collaboration with partners" — the exact language the activists already labelled greenwashing. McCartney doesn't need rhetoric; she needs to point. She built something coveted without the dirty shortcut, which means her existence is the proof his excuses don't need to be true. Arroyo defends the bottle the whole world loves and the ocean is choking on; McCartney just has to ask why those are the same object. No room at a creativity festival scores that for Coke.

04The Crowd

The Creators vs. The Creatives

The festival's deepest fault line, finally in one ring: the two cultures that spent all week eyeing each other across the Croisette. Mann reaches more humans before breakfast than most Grand Prix winners reach in a year — with stories the craft establishment won't call advertising, or even good. Sobhani runs creative for one of the world's most awarded networks and has spent a career on the idea that reach without craft is just noise at scale. Volume's new empire vs. the keepers of the craft.

Red corner
DEF.Dhar Mann
Dhar Mann Studios · Founder
The Creator
Billions of views · zero Lions, zero given
VS
Blue corner
Chaka SobhaniDEF.

The Creative

Forcing QMann gets a billion views on work this room would never award. Sobhani gives Lions to work most of those billion will never see. So settle it: is creativity what moves the most people, or what moves them most — and which of you is actually scared the other one's right?
Sobhani def. Mann — on the harder craft
Method · split, won the room
Argument SobhaniForcing Q MannThe Room Sobhani

Mann answers the forcing question better — he can put a billion real humans on the table and ask Sobhani to match it, and "they'll never see your Lion" is a punch that lands clean. He genuinely wins the half of the argument about scale, and he should: nobody in that room reaches what he reaches. But the room is the room — a creativity festival, scoring craft, packed with people whose entire identity is that there's a difference between attention and art. Sobhani doesn't have to out-reach him; she has to make the case that moving someone deeply is a harder, rarer thing than moving them briefly, and she's spent a career proving it. Mann owns the scoreboard; Sobhani owns the definition — and at Cannes, the definition is the home crowd. The honest read: he wins the internet and she wins the building, and both of them leave quietly afraid the other one is the future.
